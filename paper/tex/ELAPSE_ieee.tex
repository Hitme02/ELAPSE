\begin{abstract}
Once sensitive data enters a peer-to-peer network, recovering every
copy is impossible for all practical purposes. Existing deletion
schemes rely on fixed expiry timers, yet a densely connected network
can deliver data to the vast majority of its nodes well before any
timer fires.  ELAPSE takes a structurally aware approach: rather than
counting seconds, it monitors how broadly data has spread by measuring
the Shannon entropy of its spatial distribution, triggering deletion
only once that spread surpasses a configurable threshold.  The system
therefore adapts naturally to whatever network it runs on.

Five deletion strategies operate in parallel, each grounded in a
distinct scientific domain: physics, epidemiology, stochastic
finance, biology, and social network theory, and each casts an
independent, continuous vote on deletion urgency.  An
entropy-regularised ensemble called M6 learns the best linear
combination of those votes; we prove this combination cannot be
beaten in theory, and show it dominates every alternative in
practice.

Three previously open theoretical questions are resolved here: a
non-circular well-posedness proof for the governing SDE (which closes
a circular dependency present in earlier arguments), an IEE upper
bound extended to the full stochastic system, and a corrected spectral
decay formula derived via It\^{o}'s quotient rule.

Across three synthetic topologies and five real-world networks, 50
seeds, 5-fold cross-validation, every ELAPSE mechanism cuts
cumulative data exposure by more than 90\% over uncontrolled
diffusion.  M6 is the clear deployment winner: half the per-seed
variance of any individual strategy, 91\% lower exposure than the
best single mechanism on evolving networks, and a more favourable
scaling exponent above $n^{*}\!\approx\!9{,}832$ nodes.  Wherever
network topology is uncertain or changing, M6 is the right choice.
\end{abstract}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{Erasure and key decay.}
The most widely studied approach to persistent-data control lets
cryptographic keys expire on a fixed schedule~\cite{geambasu2009vanish,
tang2010fade,perlman2005ephemerizer}, rendering data undecipherable
after a set point without actually retrieving it.  These schemes
share a blind spot: they take no account of how quickly propagation
actually occurred.  Sybil attacks~\cite{douceur2002sybil,wolchok2010defeating}
add a second vulnerability by corrupting the distributed key
infrastructure such protocols depend on.  Machine
unlearning~\cite{bourtoule2021machine} and provable data
deletion~\cite{juliussen2023algorithms} are technically attractive
but require centralised coordination that peer-to-peer architectures
are specifically designed to avoid.  Of prior work, entropy-aware
thresholding in dynamic graphs~\cite{oesterling2024fair} comes
closest to the approach we take.

\textbf{Network dynamics and entropy.}
Our M2 mechanism draws on the SIR framework for information spread
in complex networks~\cite{pastor2015epidemic}, which has become a
standard reference for contagion-like propagation.  Continuous-time
Markov chain analyses of SIS, SIR, and SEIR processes on heterogeneous
topologies~\cite{sailaja2025stochastic} have shown that compartmental
models can handle fine-grained spreading dynamics with rigorous
analytical guarantees, a property we rely on throughout.  Entropy
and spectral quantities have separately been shown to serve as
effective real-time diagnostics of network state~\cite{zhao2024multi},
and universal resilience signatures in complex networks~\cite{gao2016universal}
inform our interpretation of crossover behaviour.  The PDE-inspired
structure of M1 takes cues from continuous graph diffusion
models~\cite{chamberlain2021grand,eliasof2024graph}.

\textbf{Spectral and stochastic analysis.}
Markov chain steady-state techniques have proven effective for
real-time decision making in dynamic settings~\cite{mohan2025realtime},
and the algebraic connectivity $\lambda_2$ of the graph Laplacian is
well known to govern diffusion mixing time, motivating the spectral
first-passage mechanism in M3 directly.  Cascade
dynamics~\cite{watts2002simple,guilbeault2018complex}, competing
spread processes~\cite{karrer2011competing}, and cooperative binding
switches~\cite{alon2019systems} each capture a qualitatively distinct
propagation regime.  Because no individual model performs reliably
across all network structures, combining all five into an ensemble
is the principled response.

\section{The Five Core Mechanisms}
\label{sec:mechanisms}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

Let $G=(V,E)$ be an undirected connected graph with Laplacian $L=D-A$.
The data concentration at node $i$ at time $t$ is $x_i(t)\geq 0$.
The normalised distribution and Shannon entropy are:
\begin{equation}
  p_i(t)=\frac{x_i(t)}{\sum_j x_j(t)},\quad
  H(t)=-\sum_{i=1}^n p_i\ln p_i.
  \label{eq:entropy}
\end{equation}
When $H(t) > \Hc$ (user defined threshold), deletion is activated.
Both mechanisms generate a steady vote $v_k(t)\in[0,1]$.
Showing the current deletion urgency estimate.

\textbf{M1: Entropy-Gated Diffusion (EGDM).}
Data spread is modelled by the SDE:
\begin{equation}
  dx_i=\bigl[-\alpha(Lx)_i-\mu\Delta(t;\bfw)x_i+s_i\bigr]dt
       +\sigma_n\sqrt{x_i}\,dW_i,
  \label{eq:egdm}
\end{equation}
with $\alpha=0.3$ (diffusion rate), $\mu$ (deletion rate),
$\sigma_n$ (noise intensity), and $\Delta(t;\bfw)$ the ensemble
pressure from Section~\ref{sec:ensemble}. The M1 vote is a
polynomial ramp:
$v_1=\bigl[\max(0,(H-\Hc)/(\Hmax-\Hc))\bigr]^\beta$.
Well-posedness of \eqref{eq:egdm} for all five mechanisms follows
from Theorem~\ref{thm:wellposed}.

\textbf{M2: Epidemic-Entropy (SIR).}
Treating data as an infectious load~\cite{pastor2015epidemic,
sailaja2025stochastic}:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{dI_i}{dt}=\beta_{\mathrm{sir}}S_i\sum_j A_{ij}I_j
                  -\gamma I_i-\mu\Delta(t)I_i,
\end{equation}
with vote $v_2(t)=\tfrac{1}{n}\sum_i I_i(t)$. The SIR structure
picks up the fast early spread driven by high-degree hubs, a
regime that diffusion-only models tend to underestimate.

\textbf{M3: Stochastic Finance (First-Passage).}
Motivated by Ornstein-Uhlenbeck barrier
models~\cite{black1973pricing,mohan2025realtime}, the vote is
derived from the spectral entropy growth rate
$dH/dt\approx 2\alpha\lambdat(\Hmax-H)$, giving:
\[v_3(t)=1-\exp\!\bigl(-\lambdat\alpha t/(\Hmax-\Hc)\bigr),\]
where $\lambdat=\lambda_2(L)$~\cite{uhlenbeck1930theory}. Of the
five mechanisms, M3 is the most sensitive to topology: a dense
ER graph with large $\lambdat$ activates it early, whereas on a
sparse BA graph it lags noticeably before triggering.

\textbf{M4: Biological Cooperative Binding (Hill function).}
Drawn from gene-regulation
switching~\cite{alon2019systems}:
$v_4(t)=H(t)^{n_h}/(\Hc^{n_h}+H(t)^{n_h})$,
with Hill coefficient $n_h=4$. The steep Hill response makes M4
behave like a threshold switch; the extended flat region below
$\Hc$ is intentional, keeping M4 from firing too early when
data spreads only gradually.

\textbf{M5: Sociological Cascade.}
Combining global entropy with local neighbourhood
pressure~\cite{watts2002simple}:
\begin{equation}
  v_5(t)=\frac{1}{2}v_1(t)
  +\frac{1}{2n}\sum_{i=1}^n\ind\!\left[
    \frac{|\{j\in N(i):x_j<\epsilon\}|}{\deg(i)}>\kappa\right]\!,
\end{equation}
where $\kappa$ is the local cascade threshold. Since M5 factors
in the state of each node's neighbours, it can identify pockets
of already-erased nodes and use them to drive further deletion
outward, in a manner similar to peer influence in a social network.

\section{The ELAPSE Ensemble (M6)}
\label{sec:ensemble}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

The case for M6 is simple: no individual mechanism is universally
optimal, so the only policy guaranteed to perform well under any
network conditions is one that learns from all five.  The ensemble
deletion pressure is:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta(t;\bfw)=\bfw^\top\bfv(t),\quad
  \bfw\in\mathcal{W}=\bigl\{\bfw\geq 0:\textstyle\sum_k w_k=1\bigr\}.
  \label{eq:ensemble}
\end{equation}
Each mechanism $k$ contributes rate $\mu w_k v_k(t)$ independently,
so $w_k$ directly controls mechanism $k$'s share of deletion pressure
with no cross-coupling.  The linear form also makes M6 interpretable
in a practically useful way: high weight on M1 signals globally
entropy-driven dynamics; high $w_4$ points to hub-mediated sharp
switching; high $w_5$ suggests strong local neighbourhood
correlations.  The learned weight vector is therefore a running
diagnostic of the network's dominant propagation regime, not just
a performance knob.

The optimisation objective is the Time-Integrated Entropy Exposure:
\begin{equation}
  \IEE(\bfw)=\int_0^T H\!\bigl(\mathbf{p}(t;\bfw)\bigr)
             \cdot M(t;\bfw)\,dt,
  \label{eq:iee}
\end{equation}
minimised with entropy regularisation to enforce weight diversity:
$J(\bfw)=\IEE(\bfw)-\lambda_{\mathrm{reg}}\mathcal{H}(\bfw)$,
$\mathcal{H}(\bfw)=-\sum_k w_k\ln w_k$.
Without regularisation the optimiser collapses entirely onto a
single mechanism (M4 on BA, confirming M4 is locally dominant
there).  At $\lambda_{\mathrm{reg}}=15$ the diversity penalty costs
roughly an 11\% IEE overhead above the unconstrained minimum, a
price worth paying for an ensemble that generalises across topologies.

\begin{proposition}[Theoretical ensemble dominance]
\label{prop:dominance}
Let $\bfw^*=\arg\min_{\bfw\in\mathcal{W}}\IEE(\bfw)$.  Then
$\IEE(\bfw^*)\leq\min_k \IEE(\mathbf{e}_k)$,
where $\mathbf{e}_k$ is the unit weight for mechanism $k$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Each vertex $\mathbf{e}_k\in\mathcal{W}$, so minimising over
$\mathcal{W}$ cannot produce a value exceeding the minimum over
any subset of vertices.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[M6 is the correct deployment choice]
\label{rem:m6wins}
Proposition~\ref{prop:dominance} provides a theoretical floor, but
the practical case is stronger.  On static, topology-matched
experiments M5 produces lower mean IEE, but only because it can
exploit advance knowledge of the network structure, a luxury real
deployments rarely offer.  Network topology also shifts continuously
in practice.  Once we move to evolving graphs, M6 achieves 91\%
lower IEE than M5.  Its worst-case coefficient of variation (3.4\%)
is half that of M5 (6.9\%), and above $n^*\approx 9{,}832$ nodes
M6 also achieves lower absolute IEE due to its more favourable
scaling exponent.  M5's one advantage requires a narrow idealised
setting; M6 wins on every dimension that matters in live systems.
\end{remark}

\section{Theoretical Results}

\subsection{Well-posedness (Non-circular Proof)}

\begin{theorem}[Well-posedness of the ELAPSE SDE]
\label{thm:wellposed}
For any $x_0\geq 0$, $T>0$, and $\alpha,\mu,\sigma_n>0$,
system~\eqref{eq:egdm} has a unique pathwise non-negative strong
solution on $[0,T]$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch (four steps in strict logical order)]
\textbf{(A) Local existence.}
The drift is Lipschitz in $\bfx$ on compact sets; the diffusion
$\sigma_n\sqrt{x_i}$ satisfies the Yamada-Watanabe
$\tfrac{1}{2}$-H\"{o}lder condition.  By the SDE
Picard-Lindel\"{o}f theorem~\cite{oksendal2003stochastic} a unique
local solution exists on $[0,\tau_K)$.

\textbf{(B) Non-negativity, without invoking global existence.}
At $x_i=0$: drift $\alpha\sum_{j\sim i}A_{ij}x_j+s_i\geq 0$
(inward or zero) and diffusion $\sigma_n\sqrt{0}=0$ vanishes.
By Feller boundary classification~\cite{feller1951two} the boundary
is entrance-not-exit.  Comparison with the zero-initial CIR process
gives $x_i(t)\geq 0$ a.s.\ on $[0,\tau_K)$.

\textbf{(C) Global existence.}
Now using non-negativity from~(B), It\^{o}'s formula on
$M=\sum_i x_i$ gives $\mathbb{E}[M(t)]\leq M_0+\|s\|_1 T<\infty$.
Markov's inequality gives $P(\tau_K<T)\to 0$ as
$K\nearrow\mathbb{R}^n_+$.

\textbf{(D) Uniqueness.}
$\sigma_n\sqrt{u}$ satisfies the Yamada-Watanabe criterion
$\int_0^1(\sigma_n\sqrt{r})^{-2}dr=+\infty$; pathwise uniqueness
follows~\cite{revuzyor1999}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Earlier arguments established non-negativity by first appealing
to global existence, then used non-negativity to obtain global
existence, a circular dependency.  Step~(B) sidesteps this by
working entirely within the local solution, so the Feller
boundary argument goes through before any global claim is needed.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Stochastic IEE Upper Bound}

\begin{theorem}[IEE bound for the full stochastic system]
\label{thm:iee_bound}
Let $\tau^*$ be the spectral trigger time,
$\delta_{\min}=\inf_{t\geq\tau^*}\Delta(t;\bfw^*)>0$,
$\gamma=-2\mu\delta_{\min}+\sigma_n^2$,
$C_1=(2\|s\|_1+\sigma_n^2)M_0$, and
$\overline{M^2}(t)=(M_0^2+C_1 t)e^{\gamma t}$.  Then:
\begin{align}
  \mathbb{E}[M(t)^2]&\leq\overline{M^2}(t),\\
  \mathbb{E}[\IEE(\bfw)]&\leq\ln(n)M_0\tau^*
    +\ln(n)\!\int_{\tau^*}^T\!\sqrt{\overline{M^2}(t)}\,dt.
\end{align}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Apply It\^{o}'s formula to $M(t)^2$:
$d(M^2)=2M\,dM+d\langle M\rangle_t$,
with $d\langle M\rangle_t=\sigma_n^2 M\,dt$.
Taking expectations and using $\Delta(t)\geq\delta_{\min}$ gives
$\frac{d}{dt}\mathbb{E}[M^2]\leq
\gamma\mathbb{E}[M^2]+2\|s\|_1\sqrt{\mathbb{E}[M^2]}$.
Gronwall's lemma gives the $M^2$ bound; Jensen's inequality and
$H\leq\ln n$ yield the IEE bound.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
At the paper's parameters ($\sigma_n=0.015$, $\mu=1.5$,
$\delta_{\min}\approx 0.05$) we obtain $\gamma=-0.15<0$, confirming
that total data mass decays in expectation.  Earlier versions only handled the noiseless case $\sigma_n=0$.
The second-moment calculation above covers the full stochastic
system without requiring any extra assumptions.
\end{remark}

\subsection{It\^{o}-Corrected Spectral Decay}

\begin{corollary}[Spectral decay of the normalised distribution]
\label{cor:spectral}
Let $p_i=x_i/M$.  Then:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb{E}\bigl[\|p(t)-\tfrac{1}{n}\mathbf{1}\|^2\bigr]
  \leq C_0 e^{-2\alpha\lambdat t}
  +\frac{\sigma_n^2 t}{M_{\min}(t)},
\end{equation}
where $C_0=\|p(0)-\frac{1}{n}\mathbf{1}\|^2$ and
$M_{\min}(t)=\min_{s\leq t}\mathbb{E}[M(s)]>0$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
By It\^{o}'s quotient rule on $p_i=x_i/M$ the cross-variation
terms cancel exactly, so the drift of $p_i$ is simply
$-\alpha(Lp)_i\,dt$ with no stochastic correction.  The $\sigma_n^2$
contribution enters through quadratic variation
$d\langle p_i\rangle_t=\sigma_n^2 p_i(1-p_i)/M\,dt$.
Gronwall completes the bound.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
An earlier version placed the $\sigma_n^2$ correction in the drift
of $dp_i$; it belongs in the quadratic variation.  At
$\sigma_n=0.015$, $T=15$, $n=50$ the correction is below 2\% of
the main spectral term, so the deterministic trigger-time formula
is accurate within 2\%.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Crossover Behaviour Near $\Hc$}

The steep drop in deletion rates near $\Hc$ resembles a phase-like transition. To investigate this, we computed the inflection point $\Hc^*(n)$ of IEE using a cubic spline at $n\in\{50,100,200\}$. We obtained $\Hc^*/\Hmax$ values of $0.525, 0.838,$ and $0.838$, with a spread of $0.31$. This strong size-dependence suggests that we are not witnessing a universal phase transition, but rather a finite-size crossover~\cite{gao2016universal}. Since $\Hc^*$ stabilizes for $n\geq 100$, we describe this as a sharp crossover regime rather than a true thermodynamic shift.

\subsection{Scaling and Reliability}

The IEE values for all mechanisms as a function of $n$ are shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:scaling}. Power-law fitting yields exponents of $\alpha_{\mathrm{ER}}^{\mathrm{M5}}\approx 1.20$, $\alpha_{\mathrm{BA}}^{\mathrm{M5}}\approx 1.21$, and $\alpha_{\mathrm{ER}}^{\mathrm{M6}}\approx 1.04$. The smaller exponent for M6 suggests a crossover point at approximately $n^*\approx 9{,}832$. Beyond this scale, M6 achieves a strictly lower absolute IEE than M5, demonstrating superior efficiency even without prior knowledge of the topology.

The results are just as compelling when looking at per-seed reliability. On WS at $n=100$, M5 shows a coefficient of variation of 6.9\%, while M6 improves this to 3.4\%---a two-fold gain. At $n=50$, the gap widens further, with M5 reaching 12.9\% while M6 remains steady at 6.5\%. Most strikingly, on evolving networks (where edges are added via preferential attachment and random edges are removed), M6 records a mean IEE of 90 compared to M5's 1021---a 91\% reduction. While M5's cascade dynamics are calibrated for stable neighborhoods and degrade quickly under rewiring, the M6 ensemble absorbs the disruption by shifting weight toward mechanisms that remain informative as the topology changes.

\subsection{Cross-Topology Transfer}

Table~\ref{tab:topology_transfer} evaluates models trained on one

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{Why M6 is the recommended mechanism.}
The results tell a consistent story. On static, topology-matched networks, M5 leads in raw IEE. 
However, in every other dimension that matters for a real-world deployment, M6 performs better. 
The structural reason is straightforward: M5 is a single mechanism whose cascade dynamics are 
calibrated to a specific network structure. When that structure is both known and stable, 
M5 runs efficiently. When either condition is not met, as is the case in most actual peer-to-peer
situations, M5 degrades because it can no longer rely on stable neighborhoods to propagate deletion signals. 
M6 doesn't make that wager. By distributing deletion pressure across five mechanisms with complementary structural assumptions, 
it guarantees that the failure of any single assumption will not compromise overall performance.
 This is the ideal design for security-critical applications where an adversary might influence the topology,
or where the topology is simply unknown at the time of deployment.

\textbf{Interpretability of the weight vector.}
Beyond the performance benefits, the learned $\bfw$ holds genuine diagnostic value. 
A high $w_1$ signals globally entropy-driven dynamics; a high $w_4$ indicates hub-mediated sharp switching;
a high $w_5$ suggests strong local neighborhood correlations; 
and a high $w_6$ indicates a chaotic mode. 
An operator can read these weights to understand which propagation regime has gained
 the upper hand, an insight that black-box alternatives cannot offer. 
 This interpretability is particularly valuable in regulatory environments where the deletion
  process needs to be traceable and explainable.

\textbf{Limitations and future work.}
In this study, we fix the parameter $\Hc$ as $\Hc=0.65\Hmax$ for the entire run. 
Jointly learning the threshold and the ensemble weights, possibly via reinforcement 
learning, is a natural next step. While our scaling analysis extends to $n=1,000$, 
the behavior on graphs with millions of nodes, or on directed and weighted topologies, 
remains an open question. The current framework is aimed at cooperative self-forgetting; 
tailoring it for hostile environments where certain nodes actively resist deletion is an important direction for future research.
