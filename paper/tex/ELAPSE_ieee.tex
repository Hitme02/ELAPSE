\begin{abstract}
We introduce ELAPSE, a framework for active data containment in
peer-to-peer networks that replaces fixed-timer erasure with an
entropy-based trigger.  As data spreads, the Shannon entropy of its
spatial distribution rises; when entropy crosses a chosen threshold
$\Hc$, ELAPSE activates deletion.  Five deletion mechanisms drawn from
physics, epidemiology, biology, stochastic finance, and sociology cast
independent votes, and an entropy-regularised ensemble (M6) learns
their optimal weights.

We prove three new theoretical results.  \emph{(i)} Well-posedness of
the ELAPSE SDE is established via a non-circular proof: non-negativity
is proved using Feller boundary classification before, not after, global
existence.  \emph{(ii)} The IEE upper bound is extended to the full
stochastic system ($\sigma_n > 0$) via a second-moment Gronwall
argument; the decay condition requires $\sigma_n^2 < 2\mu\delta_{\min}$,
satisfied at all tested parameters.  \emph{(iii)} The spectral decay
corollary is corrected via It\^{o}'s quotient rule: the $\sigma_n^2$
correction enters through the quadratic variation of $p_i = x_i/M$,
not its drift, and is numerically $<2\%$ of the main term.

Simulations with 5-fold cross-validation ($N=50$ seeds) on three
canonical topologies show all mechanisms reduce IEE by $>90\%$ over
uncontrolled diffusion.  The social cascade mechanism (M5) achieves
the lowest per-topology IEE on static networks; M6 wins on three
complementary dimensions: (i) reliability---the worst-case
coefficient of variation across topologies is $3.4\%$ (M6)
vs.\ $6.9\%$ (M5); (ii) evolving networks---on an ER$\to$BA
rewiring sequence, M6 achieves $91\%$ lower IEE than M5; and
(iii) large-network scaling---M6's power-law exponent $\alpha=1.04$
on ER yields a crossover advantage over M5 ($\alpha=1.20$) above
$n^{*}\!\approx\!9\,832$ nodes.
Finite-size scaling analysis reveals that the
observed sharp activation at $\Hc$ is a finite-size \emph{crossover},
not a universal phase transition.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

Once sensitive data enters a peer-to-peer network it replicates
autonomously.  Protocols such as Vanish~\cite{geambasu2009vanish} and
the Ephemerizer~\cite{perlman2005ephemerizer} limit exposure by decaying
cryptographic keys on a fixed chronological
timer~\cite{douceur2002sybil,tang2010fade}.  The fundamental limitation
is that a densely connected scale-free graph can distribute data to a
large fraction of its nodes well before any fixed timeout fires.

ELAPSE replaces the chronological clock with the Shannon entropy of the
spatial data distribution as the erasure trigger.  Entropy provides a
topology-sensitive signal: it rises fast on scale-free networks (where
hub nodes spread data quickly) and more slowly on lattice-like networks.
This converts a static timer into an adaptive, environment-aware
deletion policy.

Five deletion mechanisms---drawn from physics, epidemiology, stochastic
finance, biology, and sociology---independently estimate the current
danger level and cast continuous-valued votes.  An entropy-regularised
ensemble (M6) blends these votes optimally.  Beyond the empirical
evaluation, we establish three corrected theoretical results that resolve
gaps in earlier drafts of this work and in prior literature: a
non-circular well-posedness proof, a genuinely stochastic IEE upper
bound, and an It\^{o}-corrected spectral decay corollary.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{Information diffusion and entropy on graphs.}
The epidemiological framework of Pastor-Satorras et
al.~\cite{pastor2015epidemic} established the standard SIR model for
information spread on complex networks.  Entropy-based indicators of
network state were validated by Zhao et al.~\cite{zhao2024multi}.
Continuous graph diffusion via PDE-GCN formulations (GRAND
\cite{chamberlain2021grand}; Eliasof et al.~\cite{eliasof2024graph})
and universal resilience patterns~\cite{gao2016universal} motivate the
spectral-gap analysis of Section~\ref{sec:theory}.

\textbf{Static and active containment.}
Key-decay protocols~\cite{geambasu2009vanish,tang2010fade,
perlman2005ephemerizer} suffer from topology-blindness; Sybil
vulnerabilities~\cite{wolchok2010defeating,douceur2002sybil} further
weaken them.  Machine unlearning~\cite{bourtoule2021machine} and
verifiable topological removal~\cite{juliussen2023algorithms} address
post-hoc deletion but assume centralised control.
Active-thresholding in dynamic graphs~\cite{oesterling2024fair} is the
closest prior to ELAPSE.

\textbf{Multi-mechanism ensemble methods.}
Cascade models~\cite{watts2002simple,guilbeault2018complex}, competing
epidemics~\cite{karrer2011competing}, and systems-biology
switches~\cite{alon2019systems} each capture a distinct propagation
regime.  ELAPSE combines all five to hedge against topology uncertainty.

\section{The Five Core Mechanisms}
\label{sec:mechanisms}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

Let $G = (V,E)$ be an undirected connected graph with Laplacian $L =
D - A$.  The data concentration at node $i$ at time $t$ is $x_i(t)
\geq 0$.  The normalised distribution and Shannon entropy are:
\begin{equation}
  p_i(t) = \frac{x_i(t)}{\sum_j x_j(t)},\qquad
  H(t) = -\sum_{i=1}^n p_i\ln p_i.
  \label{eq:entropy}
\end{equation}
Deletion is triggered when $H(t)$ exceeds threshold $\Hc$.
Each mechanism $k$ casts a continuous vote $v_k(t) \in [0,1]$.

\textbf{M1 -- Entropy-Gated Diffusion Model (EGDM).}
The SDE governing data spread under controlled deletion is:
\begin{equation}
  dx_i = \bigl[-\alpha(Lx)_i - \mu\Delta(t;\bfw)x_i + s_i\bigr]dt
         + \sigma_n\sqrt{x_i}\,dW_i,
  \label{eq:egdm}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha = 0.3$ is the diffusion coefficient, $\mu$ the mortality
rate, $\sigma_n$ the noise intensity, and $\Delta(t;\bfw)$ the ensemble
deletion pressure (Section~\ref{sec:ensemble}).  The M1 vote is a
polynomial switch:
$v_1(t) = \bigl[\max(0, (H(t)-\Hc)/(\Hmax-\Hc))\bigr]^\beta$.
Theorem~\ref{thm:wellposed} establishes that \eqref{eq:egdm} is
well-posed for all five mechanisms.

\textbf{M2 -- Epidemic-Entropy (Compartmental SIR).}
Data is modelled as infectious load via:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{dI_i}{dt} = \beta_{\mathrm{sir}} S_i \sum_j A_{ij}I_j
                   - \gamma I_i - \mu\Delta(t)I_i,
\end{equation}
with vote $v_2(t) = \tfrac{1}{n}\sum_i I_i(t)$~\cite{pastor2015epidemic}.

\textbf{M3 -- Stochastic-Finance (First-Passage Time).}
Motivated by barrier models for default risk~\cite{black1973pricing},
deletion is triggered via an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck first-passage
probability:
$v_3(t) = 1 - \exp(-\lambdat\,\alpha\,t / (\Hmax - \Hc))$,
where $\lambdat = \lambda_2(L)$ is the algebraic connectivity.  This
\emph{derived} vote emerges from the spectral entropy growth equation
$dH/dt \approx 2\alpha\lambdat(\Hmax - H)$,
giving effective rate $\lambda_{\rm eff} =
\alpha\lambdat(\Hmax-H)/(\Hmax-\Hc)$~\cite{uhlenbeck1930theory}.

\textbf{M4 -- Biological Cooperative Binding (Hill Function).}
Cooperative switching from systems biology~\cite{alon2019systems}:
$v_4(t) = H(t)^{n_h} / (\Hc^{n_h} + H(t)^{n_h})$,
with Hill coefficient $n_h = 4$.  This achieves a more stable
asymptotic transition than polynomial switching.

\textbf{M5 -- Sociological Cascade Pressure.}
Combining global entropy with local neighbourhood
pressure~\cite{watts2002simple}:
\begin{equation}
  v_5(t) = \tfrac{1}{2}v_1(t) + \tfrac{1}{2}\,\frac{1}{n}\!\sum_{i=1}^n
  \!\ind\!\!\left[\frac{|\{j\in N(i): x_j < \epsilon\}|}{\deg(i)} > \kappa\right],
\end{equation}
where $\kappa$ is the local cascade threshold.

\section{The ELAPSE Ensemble (M6)}
\label{sec:ensemble}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

The ensemble deletion pressure is the linear combination:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta(t;\bfw) = \bfw^\top\bfv(t), \quad
  \bfw \in \mathcal{W} = \bigl\{\bfw \geq 0 : \textstyle\sum_k w_k = 1\bigr\}.
  \label{eq:ensemble}
\end{equation}
The linear form is structurally motivated: each mechanism $k$ contributes
deletion rate $\mu w_k v_k(t)$ independently, so $w_k$ directly and
exclusively scales mechanism $k$'s contribution with no cross-terms.

The optimisation objective is the Time-Integrated Entropy Exposure:
\begin{equation}
  \IEE(\bfw) = \int_0^T H\!\bigl(\mathbf{p}(t;\bfw)\bigr)\cdot M(t;\bfw)\,dt,
  \label{eq:iee}
\end{equation}
minimised subject to entropy regularisation to prevent weight collapse:
$J(\bfw) = \IEE(\bfw) - \lambda_{\mathrm{reg}}\mathcal{H}(\bfw)$
where $\mathcal{H}(\bfw) = -\sum_k w_k\ln w_k$.

\begin{lemma}[Convexity of IEE under linearisation]
\label{lem:convex}
Under the linearisation that the state trajectory $\bfx(t;\bfw)$ is
affine in $\bfw$ (valid to first order for small $\Delta$ variations),
$\IEE(\bfw)$ is convex in $\bfw$ over $\mathcal{W}$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Under the linearisation, $M(t;\bfw)$ is affine in $\bfw$.
Treating $H$ and $M$ as jointly non-negative, their product $H \cdot M$
is convex in $\bfw$ on the simplex (product of two non-negative
approximately-affine functions), and integration over $[0,T]$ preserves
convexity.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The linearisation is an approximation: $H(t;\bfw)$ is a concave
nonlinear function of $p(t)$, which depends on $\bfw$ through the
nonlinear SDE \eqref{eq:egdm}.  Lemma~\ref{lem:convex} should therefore
be understood as providing a local convexity guarantee.  Empirically, the
IEE surface is unimodal (verified by grid search at $n=100$), so
Nelder-Mead finds a unique global minimum in practice.
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[Ensemble dominance at the theoretical optimum]
\label{prop:dominance}
Let $\bfw^* = \arg\min_{\bfw\in\mathcal{W}} \IEE(\bfw)$.  Then
$\IEE(\bfw^*) \leq \min_{k} \IEE(\mathbf{e}_k)$,
where $\mathbf{e}_k$ is the one-hot weight for mechanism $k$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Each $\mathbf{e}_k \in \mathcal{W}$, so minimising IEE over all of
$\mathcal{W}$ cannot exceed the objective at any vertex.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[M6 vs M5: when each is optimal]
Proposition~\ref{prop:dominance} guarantees the ensemble is \emph{at
least as good} as the best single mechanism at the theoretical optimum.
In practice:
(a) M5 achieves lower mean IEE on static per-topology experiments
(M5/M6 ratio $\approx 0.70$; Table~\ref{tab:iee});
(b) M6 achieves lower \emph{coefficient of variation} (CV\%)
across all tested topologies---worst-case CV $3.4\%$ (M6) vs
$6.9\%$ (M5)---making M6 the more \emph{reliable} mechanism;
(c) on evolving ER$\to$BA networks, M6's ensemble OR-gate adapts
to changing topology and achieves $91\%$ lower IEE than M5.
The ensemble's value is \emph{reliability and adaptability under
uncertainty}, not unconditional IEE dominance on static graphs.
\end{remark}

\section{Theoretical Results}

\subsection{Well-posedness (Non-circular Proof)}

\begin{theorem}[Well-posedness of the ELAPSE SDE]
\label{thm:wellposed}
For any $x_0 \geq 0$, $T > 0$, $\alpha,\mu,\sigma_n > 0$, the system
\eqref{eq:egdm} has a unique pathwise non-negative strong solution on
$[0,T]$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch (four steps in strict logical order)]
\textbf{(A) Local existence.}
The drift is Lipschitz in $\bfx$ on compact sets; the diffusion
$\sigma_n\sqrt{x_i}$ satisfies the Yamada--Watanabe
$\tfrac{1}{2}$-H\"{o}lder condition.  By the SDE Picard--Lindel\"{o}f
theorem~\cite{oksendal2003stochastic}, a unique local solution exists
on $[0,\tau_K)$.

\textbf{(B) Non-negativity on $[0,\tau_K)$, independently of global existence.}
At $x_i = 0$: drift satisfies $b_i|_{x_i=0} = \alpha\sum_{j\sim i}
A_{ij}x_j + s_i \geq 0$ (inward or zero), and diffusion
$\sigma_n\sqrt{0} = 0$ vanishes.  By Feller boundary
classification~\cite{feller1951two}, the boundary is
\emph{entrance-not-exit}.  The comparison theorem~\cite{revuzyor1999}
with the zero-initial CIR process $dY_i = \sigma_n\sqrt{Y_i}\,dW_i$,
$Y_i(0)=0$, gives $x_i(t) \geq Y_i(t) = 0$ a.s.  Non-negativity is
established \emph{without invoking global existence}.

\textbf{(C) Global existence.}
\emph{Now} using non-negativity from (B): apply It\^{o}'s formula to
$M = \sum_i x_i$,
$\mathbb{E}[M(t)] \leq M_0 + \|s\|_1 T < \infty$.
By Markov's inequality, $P(\tau_K < T) \to 0$ as $K \nearrow \mathbb{R}^n_+$.

\textbf{(D) Uniqueness.}
$\sigma_n\sqrt{u}$ satisfies the Yamada--Watanabe criterion
$\int_0^1 (\sigma_n\sqrt{r})^{-2}dr = +\infty$~\cite{revuzyor1999};
pathwise uniqueness follows.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Earlier proofs had Steps (B) and (C) reversed: global existence was
invoked to establish $M(t) > 0$, which was then used to prove
non-negativity --- a circular dependency.  Step (B) uses only the
Feller boundary argument on the local solution, breaking the cycle.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Stochastic IEE Upper Bound}

\begin{theorem}[IEE bound for the full stochastic system]
\label{thm:iee_bound}
Let $\tau^*$ be the spectral trigger time and
$\delta_{\min} = \inf_{t\geq\tau^*}\Delta(t;\bfw^*) > 0$.
Define $\gamma = -2\mu\delta_{\min} + \sigma_n^2$,
$C_1 = (2\|s\|_1 + \sigma_n^2)M_0$, and
$\overline{M^2}(t) = (M_0^2 + C_1 t)e^{\gamma t}$.
Then:
\begin{align}
  \mathbb{E}[M(t)^2] &\leq \overline{M^2}(t), \label{eq:M2_bound}\\
  \mathbb{E}[\IEE(\bfw)] &\leq \ln(n)M_0\tau^*
    + \ln(n)\!\int_{\tau^*}^T\!\sqrt{\overline{M^2}(t)}\,dt.
    \label{eq:iee_bound}
\end{align}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Apply It\^{o}'s formula to $M(t)^2$:
$d(M^2) = 2M\,dM + d\langle M\rangle_t$,
with $d\langle M\rangle_t = \sigma_n^2 M\,dt$.
Taking expectations and using $\Delta(t) \geq \delta_{\min}$:
$\frac{d}{dt}\mathbb{E}[M^2] \leq \gamma\,\mathbb{E}[M^2]
+ 2\|s\|_1\sqrt{\mathbb{E}[M^2]}$.
Gronwall's lemma gives \eqref{eq:M2_bound}.
Applying Jensen and $H \leq \ln n$ yields \eqref{eq:iee_bound}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
For the paper's parameters ($\sigma_n = 0.015$, $\mu = 1.5$,
$\delta_{\min} \approx 0.05$): $\gamma = -0.15 < 0$, confirming that
the mass decays on average.  Earlier versions of this result proved the
bound only for $\sigma_n = 0$; the second-moment argument extends it to
the full SDE without modification.
\end{remark}

\subsection{It\^{o}-Corrected Spectral Decay}

\begin{corollary}[Spectral decay of normalised distribution]
\label{cor:spectral}
Let $p_i = x_i/M$.  Then:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb{E}\bigl[\|p(t) - \tfrac{1}{n}\mathbf{1}\|^2\bigr]
  \leq C_0 e^{-2\alpha\lambdat t} + \frac{\sigma_n^2 t}{M_{\min}(t)},
  \label{eq:spectral_bound}
\end{equation}
where $C_0 = \|p(0) - \tfrac{1}{n}\mathbf{1}\|^2$ and
$M_{\min}(t) = \min_{s\leq t}\mathbb{E}[M(s)] > 0$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
By It\^{o}'s quotient rule applied to $p_i = x_i/M$, the
cross-variation terms $d\langle x_i,M\rangle_t = \sigma_n^2 x_i\,dt$
and $(x_i/M^3)d\langle M\rangle_t = \sigma_n^2 p_i/M\,dt$
\emph{cancel exactly}, so the drift of $p_i$ is $-\alpha(Lp)_i\,dt$
with no stochastic correction.  The $\sigma_n^2$ term enters
through the quadratic variation
$d\langle p_i\rangle_t = \sigma_n^2 p_i(1-p_i)/M\,dt$,
which contributes $\sigma_n^2/M$ per unit time to
$d\mathbb{E}[\|p-\tfrac{1}{n}\mathbf{1}\|^2]/dt$ via It\^{o}'s formula
on $V = \|p - \tfrac{1}{n}\mathbf{1}\|^2$.  Gronwall completes the
bound.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
An earlier version incorrectly attributed the $\sigma_n^2$ correction
to the \emph{drift} of $dp_i$.  The correction term does not appear in
the drift; it appears in the quadratic variation.  Numerically, at
$\sigma_n=0.015$, $T=15$, $n=50$: the correction is $\approx 6.75
\times 10^{-5} < 2\%$ of the main spectral term, so the trigger time
$\tau(\lambdat)$ computed from the deterministic formula is accurate
to within $2\%$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Crossover Behaviour Near $\Hc$}

The sharp change in deletion behaviour near $\Hc$ resembles a
percolation transition.  To test universality, we computed the
inflection point $\Hc^*(n)$ of IEE vs $\Hc/\Hmax$ via cubic spline for
$n \in \{50, 100, 200\}$.  Results: $\Hc^*/\Hmax = 0.525, 0.838,
0.838$ (spread $= 0.31$) --- a strong size-dependence.  This rules out
a universal phase transition; the behaviour is a finite-size
\emph{crossover}~\cite{gao2016universal}, with $\Hc^*$ converging
for $n \geq 100$.  We therefore describe the activation as a
``sharp crossover regime'' rather than a phase transition.

\subsection{Regularisation Sensitivity}

Without regularisation ($\lambda_{\mathrm{reg}} = 0$), the optimiser
collapses entirely onto M4 for BA, confirming M4 is the best single
mechanism at $n=200$ on BA.  At $\lambda_{\mathrm{reg}} = 15$, weight
diversity is enforced at a small IEE cost ($\approx 11\%$ above the
unconstrained minimum); the resulting ensemble generalises across
topologies.  Table~\ref{tab:reg} reports the trade-off.

\subsection{IEE Scaling and Reliability}

Figure~\ref{fig:scaling} shows IEE scaling with $n$.  A power-law fit
IEE $\propto n^\alpha$ gives exponents
$\alpha_{\mathrm{ER}}^{\mathrm{M5}} \approx 1.20$,
$\alpha_{\mathrm{BA}}^{\mathrm{M5}} \approx 1.21$,
and $\alpha_{\mathrm{ER}}^{\mathrm{M6}} \approx 1.04$.
M6's lower exponent on ER implies a crossover at $n^{*}\approx 9{,}832$
above which M6 achieves lower IEE than M5.

The coefficient of variation (CV $=\sigma/\mu\times100\%$) quantifies
per-seed reliability.  On Watts-Strogatz ($n=100$), M5 shows
CV\,$=6.9\%$ vs.\ M6's CV\,$=3.4\%$---a 2$\times$ improvement.
This gap widens at smaller $n$: WS $n=50$ gives M5 CV\,$=12.9\%$
vs.\ M6\,$=6.5\%$.  On evolving ER$\to$BA networks (add 2 edges by
preferential attachment, remove 2 random edges per unit time), M6
achieves mean evolving IEE\,$=90$ vs.\ M5's\,$=1021$---a 91\% reduction.

\section{Conclusions}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

ELAPSE replaces fixed erasure timers with an entropy-based trigger and
blends five physically grounded deletion mechanisms via an
entropy-regularised ensemble.  All mechanisms reduce IEE by $>90\%$
over uncontrolled diffusion.

Three previously unresolved theoretical gaps are closed: well-posedness
is established via a non-circular Feller-boundary argument; the IEE
upper bound is extended to the full stochastic system via a
second-moment Gronwall argument; and the spectral decay corollary is
corrected by attributing the $\sigma_n^2$ term to the quadratic
variation of $p_i$, not its drift.

The observed sharp activation at $\Hc$ is a finite-size crossover.
A six-metric empirical scorecard confirms that M5 is the strongest
per-topology performer on static networks; M6 wins on reliability
(worst-case CV $3.4\%$ vs $6.9\%$), evolving-network IEE ($91\%$
lower than M5), and large-network scaling (crossover $n^{*}\approx
9{,}832$ on ER).  The ensemble is the right choice when the network
topology is dynamic, the graph scale exceeds $n^{*}$, or consistent
cross-seed performance is required.  Future work includes closed-loop
optimal control via reinforcement learning and extensions to directed
graphs.
