\begin{abstract}
The restriction of information flow across stochastic, decentralized networks presents
a significant challenge for ongoing data privacy. Current cryptographic access controls
are frequently bypassed post-exfiltration, and static active containment protocols
generally rely on chronological decay sequences that do not account for the topological
realities of viral spread. In this study, we introduce the ELAPSE framework, shifting
from chronological data lifetimes toward spatial-topological containment. By measuring
the Shannon entropy of the data's spatial dispersion, ELAPSE links the mortality rate
directly to structural privacy loss. To address the failure of single-domain deletion
triggers across heterogeneous network spectra, we construct an optimization-driven
ensemble that unifies five distinct physical, epidemiological, financial, biological, and
sociological switching mechanisms. The ensemble weights are continuously resolved via an
entropy-regularised Nelder-Mead optimization to minimise the Time-Integrated Entropy
Exposure (IEE). We prove an Ensemble Dominance Proposition demonstrating that the
unregularised system mathematically bounds the best baseline deletion rate, and introduce
a regularisation parameter to ensure weight diversity. Numerical simulations spanning
Erd\H{o}s-R\'{e}nyi, Barab\'{a}si-Albert, and Watts-Strogatz networks for
$n \in [50, 500]$ confirm the framework's efficacy. While uncontained diffusion (the
theoretical baseline) resulted in exponential privacy exposure ($\mathrm{IEE} = 7055.7$
at $n = 500$), the regularised ELAPSE ensemble bounded total exposure to
$\mathrm{IEE} = 470.4$, confirming its viability for decentralized data architecture.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

Controlling the propagation of sensitive data post-distribution is a recognized
limitation in modern cryptography and network theory. Traditional authentication
structures secure data at rest. However, once a payload is accessed---whether by a
legitimate recipient, an insider threat, or a compromised endpoint---the originating
system loses physical control, allowing the data to replicate across peer-to-peer
structures natively. Attempts to limit this proliferation are generally categorized
under ``active data containment'' architectures.

Historically, protocols such as Vanish~\cite{geambasu2009vanish} and its
successors~\cite{tang2010fade} have leveraged Distributed Hash Tables (DHTs) to
continuously overwrite and decay the secret sharing keys required to decrypt a
payload. A fundamental limitation in this operational logic is its strict reliance on
chronological time. Data lifetimes are typically hardcoded to decay over a static
timeout period (e.g., 8 hours). In a highly connected scale-free graph, viral diffusion
can compromise significant portions of the network in a fraction of that baseline,
rendering temporal decay oblivious to the actual physical distribution occurring on the
network.

We propose that data lifetimes should not be static; they must be a dynamic function
of the environment. The ELAPSE framework operationalises this approach. Instead of a
chronological clock, ELAPSE uses the Shannon entropy of the spatial data concentration
as its metric, treating structural dispersion as the proximate cause for data mortality.

Furthermore, recognizing that simple diffusion thresholds may fail across different
network clustering coefficients, ELAPSE constructs a mathematically constrained ensemble
blending governing equations from distinct natural domains (epidemiology, stochastic
finance, systems biology). Section~\ref{sec:related} provides a literature review of
recent advances in graph containment. Section~\ref{sec:mechanisms} details the five
core mechanisms. Section~\ref{sec:ensemble} outlines the ELAPSE ensemble and provides
the algebraic proofs guiding the optimization. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the
empirical phase transitions mapped across varying topologies.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

The study of information containment spans several active research domains, particularly
at the intersection of geometric deep learning, network science, and privacy-preserving
architectures. Recent developments in reaction-diffusion equations on graphs have
provided sophisticated tools for modelling continuous-time dynamics on discrete
structures.

\textbf{Information Diffusion and Entropy.} The foundational mechanics of information
spread on complex networks were early formalized by epidemiological
models~\cite{pastor2015epidemic}. More recently, entropy-based metrics have emerged as
robust topological indicators. Zhao et al.\ (2024)~\cite{zhao2024multi} demonstrated
that entropy spreading provides a highly accurate macroscopic view of information
volatility across heterogeneous node degrees, establishing Shannon entropy as a primary
indicator for outbreak detection. Concurrently, advances in continuous graph diffusion,
tracing back to foundational signal processing on
graphs~\cite{shuman2013emerging} and extending to modern PDE-GCN formulations including
GRAND~\cite{chamberlain2021grand} and Eliasof et al.\ (2024)~\cite{eliasof2024graph},
have shown that applying partial differential equations (PDEs) directly to graph
topologies can accurately model non-linear propagation boundaries. The structural
resilience of such networks under perturbation is further characterized by universal
resilience patterns~\cite{gao2016universal}, which inform the scale-stress analysis in
Section~\ref{sec:results}.

\textbf{Static Data Containment.} Early containment protocols focused heavily on
cryptographic key decay. Vanish~\cite{geambasu2009vanish} introduced the concept of
leveraging DHT churn to systematically lose decryption keys, though it was quickly
shown to be vulnerable to Sybil attacks~\cite{wolchok2010defeating}. Subsequent
iterations~\cite{tang2010fade, perlman2005ephemerizer} sought to harden the storage
layers, but fundamentally retained chronological decay timers~\cite{douceur2002sybil}.

\textbf{Active Containment and Unlearning.} In the broader context of active data
containment, recent literature has pivoted toward algorithmic unlearning and adaptive
exposure limits. Bourtoule et al.\ (2021)~\cite{bourtoule2021machine} established the
foundations of machine unlearning, while Juliussen et al.\
(2023)~\cite{juliussen2023algorithms} highlighted the necessity of verifiable
topological removal protocols, and Oesterling et al.\ (2024)~\cite{oesterling2024fair}
advanced the use of active thresholding constraints in dynamic graph learning.
Furthermore, multi-agent modeling literature has increasingly adopted concepts from
systems biology~\cite{alon2019systems} and social cascades~\cite{watts2002simple} to
model robust, step-like phase transitions in complex
networks~\cite{guilbeault2018complex}. The interaction of competing information processes
on the same network topology~\cite{karrer2011competing} further motivates ensemble
approaches that account for heterogeneous propagation regimes.

The ELAPSE framework synthesizes these domains, utilizing the entropy metrics established
by~\cite{zhao2024multi} alongside the continuous diffusion mechanics
of~\cite{eliasof2024graph} to replace chronological key decay with spatial self-erasure.

\section{The 5 Core Mechanisms}
\label{sec:mechanisms}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

Let the complex network be defined by an undirected graph $G = (V, E)$ of size $n$,
evaluated via normalized adjacency $A$ and weighted graph Laplacian $L$. The active data
concentration holding at node $i$ at time $t$ is $x_i(t) \geq 0$. Instantaneous
spatial privacy exposure is derived empirically from the Shannon entropy:
\begin{equation}
  p_i(t) = \frac{x_i(t)}{\sum_j x_j(t)}, \quad
  H(t) = -\sum_{i=1}^{n} p_i(t)\ln\!\bigl(p_i(t)\bigr)
  \label{eq:entropy}
\end{equation}

Total uncontained exposure represents $H_{\max} = \ln(n)$, whereas self-erasure seeks
to enforce structural mortality whenever $H(t)$ surpasses a bounded privacy tolerance
$H_c$. The 5 foundational mechanisms independently conceptualize how to breach this
threshold, casting continuous operational votes $v_k(t) \in [0, 1]$.

\subsection{Mechanism 1: Entropy-Gated Diffusion Model (EGDM)}

The physical baseline treats data strictly via macroscopic diffusion. The continuous
state vector $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ propagates against a global polynomial
switch $\sigma(H(t))$:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt} = -\alpha L\mathbf{x} - \mu\sigma(H(t))\mathbf{x}
                           + \mathbf{s}(t) + dW_t
  \label{eq:egdm}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha = 0.3$ represents the diffusion medium against the standard Laplacian.
The vote acts polynomially out to a sharpness bound $\beta$:
\begin{equation}
  v_1(t) = \left[\max\!\left(0,\;\frac{H(t) - H_c}{H_{\max} - H_c}\right)\right]^{\!\beta}
  \label{eq:v1}
\end{equation}
Assuming the standard topological coefficients, the continuous SDE systems framing these
foundational diffusion mechanisms satisfy local Lipschitz continuity, guaranteeing unique
strong solutions up until the self-erasure bounding threshold according to standard
existence criteria~\cite{oksendal2003stochastic}.

\subsection{Mechanism 2: Epidemic-Entropy (Compartmental SIR)}

Epidemiology models data flow as infectious load~\cite{pastor2015epidemic}. Nodes
transition via Susceptible-Infected-Recovered mechanics, bypassing physical diffusion.
\begin{equation}
  \frac{dI_i}{dt} = \beta_{\mathrm{sir}} S_i \sum_j A_{ij} I_j
                   - \gamma I_i - \mu\sigma(H(t))I_i
  \label{eq:sir}
\end{equation}
This mechanism relies on local topological density. Its vote correlates to the current
severity of the outbreak: $v_2(t) = \tfrac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n I_i(t)$.

\subsection{Mechanism 3: Stochastic-Finance (First-Passage Time)}

Deriving from barrier models used to price quantitative
derivatives~\cite{black1973pricing}, the drift serves as a mean-reverting
Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (OU) process~\cite{uhlenbeck1930theory}. Within this financial
analogy, information exposure risk behaves analogously to a stochastic asset approaching
a ruin boundary: both systems exhibit a mean-reverting drift with an absorbing boundary,
making first-passage time the natural mortality trigger. Mortality is triggered
probabilistically against a first-passage time equation:
\begin{equation}
  d\mathbf{x} = \theta(\mu_{ou} - \mathbf{x})\,dt + \sigma\,dW_t
               - \mu P(\tau \leq t)\,\mathbf{x}\,dt
  \label{eq:finance}
\end{equation}
The spectral geometry of the network explicitly governs the vote limit:
$v_3(t) = 1 - \exp(-\lambda t)$ where $\lambda \propto (\alpha\lambda_2)$. Here,
$\lambda_2$ explicitly defines the graph Fiedler value---the second smallest eigenvalue
of the normalized graph Laplacian used to quantify algebraic connectivity.

\subsection{Mechanism 4: Biological Cooperative Binding (Hill Function)}

In systems biology, cellular transitions bypass step-functions via cooperative binding
constants~\cite{alon2019systems}.
\begin{equation}
  v_4(t) = \sigma_{\mathrm{hill}}(H) = \frac{H(t)^{n_h}}{H_c^{n_h} + H(t)^{n_h}}
  \label{eq:hill}
\end{equation}
Using a Hill coefficient $n_h = 4$, this formulation establishes a more
Lyapunov-stable asymptotic transition compared to polynomial fracturing.

\subsection{Mechanism 5: Sociological Cascade Pressure}

Sociological information cascades parameterize agents behaving reactively based on
localized sub-graph density~\cite{watts2002simple}.
\begin{equation}
  v_5(t) = \frac{1}{2}\sigma(H(t)) + \frac{1}{2}\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}
  \!\left(\ind\!\!\left[\frac{1}{\deg(i)}
  \sum_{j \in N(i)}\!\ind(x_j < \epsilon) > \kappa\right]\!\right)
  \label{eq:social}
\end{equation}
A localized secondary pressure exists if an arbitrary node $i$ observes that a ratio of
its immediate neighbours surpassing $\kappa$ have successfully executed self-deletion,
where $\epsilon$ represents the deletion concentration threshold below which a node is
considered to have completely erased its payload.

\section{The ELAPSE Ensemble Construction (Mechanism 6)}
\label{sec:ensemble}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

The ELAPSE engine (designated as the M6 ensemble) synthesizes the 5 constituent
mechanisms into a unified operational vector
$\mathbf{v}(t) = [v_1 \ldots v_5]^\top \in [0,1]^5$. When evaluated alongside an
uncontained theoretical baseline (M0), this creates 7 total structural configurations.
The terminal deletion pressure $\Delta(t)$ is evaluated as a strictly convex
combination:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta(t) = \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{v}(t), \quad
  \sum_{k=1}^{5} w_k = 1,\; w_k \geq 0
  \label{eq:ensemble}
\end{equation}

Optimising this projection across unknown network sizes requires minimizing the primary
functional tracking metric---the Time-Integrated Entropy Exposure:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{IEE}(\mathbf{w}) = \int_0^T H\!\bigl(\mathbf{p}(t;\mathbf{w})\bigr)
                              \cdot M(t;\mathbf{w})\, dt
  \label{eq:iee}
\end{equation}
where $M(t;\mathbf{w}) = \sum_{i=1}^n x_i(t)$ represents the aggregate surviving data
mass remaining in the network at time $t$.

A structural limitation in unconstrained Nelder-Mead simplex optimization is absolute
vertex collapse ($\mathbf{w} \to \mathbf{e}_k$). In highly volatile generative graphs,
the minimum bound naturally defaults to selecting the single most aggressive algorithm
and nullifying the others, negatively impacting out-of-distribution generalisation.

To address this, we inject Shannon regularisation directly into the objective function:
\begin{equation}
  \min_{\mathbf{w}}\; J(\mathbf{w}) = \mathrm{IEE}(\mathbf{w}) - \lambda_{\mathrm{reg}}\mathcal{H}(\mathbf{w})
  \label{eq:objective}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{H}(\mathbf{w}) = -\sum_{k=1}^{5} w_k \ln(w_k)$ functions as the
entropic spread of the weight layer. The justification for $\lambda_{\mathrm{reg}}$
scaling is discussed empirically in Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity}. By subtracting this
penalty term, the optimization process avoids the 1-hot geometric corners of the
simplex, ensuring a multi-domain strategy that retains generalization. The computational
overhead of this projection is notably minimal; the Nelder-Mead optimization natively
scales efficiently at $\mathcal{O}(K^2 \cdot S)$~\cite{gao2012implementing}, where
$K = 5$ represents the mechanisms and $S$ the aggregate discrete simulation steps
evaluating the objective.

\subsection{Theoretical Dominance Proof}

We first establish a convexity lemma for the IEE objective, which underpins both the
dominance result and the validity of global optimization via Nelder-Mead on this
surface.

\begin{lemma}[Convexity of IEE in $\mathbf{w}$]
Let the deletion pressure be the affine map $\Delta(t) = \mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{v}(t)$,
with $\mathbf{v}(t) \geq 0$ fixed for a given trajectory. Then $\mathrm{IEE}(\mathbf{w})$
is convex in $\mathbf{w}$ over the simplex $\mathcal{W}$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Fix any two weight vectors $\mathbf{w}_a, \mathbf{w}_b \in \mathcal{W}$ and
$\theta \in [0,1]$. The convex combination
$\mathbf{w}_\theta = \theta\mathbf{w}_a + (1-\theta)\mathbf{w}_b$ induces deletion
pressure $\Delta_\theta(t) = \mathbf{w}_\theta^\top\mathbf{v}(t)$. Since $\Delta(t)$
enters the state dynamics of Eq.~\eqref{eq:egdm} linearly through the erasure term
$-\mu\sigma(H)\mathbf{x}$, the resulting state trajectory $\mathbf{x}(t;\mathbf{w})$
is affine in $\mathbf{w}$ at each $t$ under this linearisation. The surviving mass
$M(t;\mathbf{w}) = \sum_i x_i(t)$ is therefore affine in $\mathbf{w}$ as a
non-negative linear functional of $\mathbf{x}$. Shannon entropy $H(t;\mathbf{w})$ is a
concave function of the normalised concentrations $p_i = x_i/\sum_j x_j$, but
$H \cdot M$ is bounded and non-negative; treating both $H$ and $M$ as non-negative
quantities, their product is convex in $\mathbf{w}$ by the fact that a product of two
non-negative affine functions is convex on the simplex. Integration over $[0,T]$
preserves convexity, yielding
$\mathrm{IEE}(\mathbf{w}_\theta) \leq \theta\,\mathrm{IEE}(\mathbf{w}_a) +
(1-\theta)\,\mathrm{IEE}(\mathbf{w}_b)$.\hfill$\square$
\end{proof}

Lemma~1 ensures that any local minimum found by the Nelder-Mead simplex solver over
$\mathcal{W}$ is also the global minimum, guaranteeing that $\mathbf{w}_{\mathrm{opt}}$
is well-defined and uniquely attained (up to ties on flat regions of the objective
surface).

\begin{proposition}[Ensemble Dominance]
Let $\mathcal{W} = \{\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^K \mid \mathbf{w} \geq 0,
\|\mathbf{w}\|_1 = 1\}$ be the probability simplex. Taking $\mathbf{e}_k \in \mathcal{W}$
as the one-hot vertex corresponding to the $k$-th single-mechanism policy, and
$\mathbf{w}_{\mathrm{opt}} = \arg\min_{\mathbf{w} \in \mathcal{W}} \mathrm{IEE}(\mathbf{w})$
as the globally optimal ensemble weights under the unregularised objective:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{IEE}(\mathbf{w}_{\mathrm{opt}}) \leq \min_{k \in \{1\ldots K\}} \mathrm{IEE}(\mathbf{e}_k)
  \label{eq:dominance}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since $\mathcal{W}$ is a compact convex set and IEE is continuous (being a finite
integral of bounded measurable functions), the minimum over $\mathcal{W}$ is attained.
By Lemma~1, IEE is convex over $\mathcal{W}$, so $\mathbf{w}_{\mathrm{opt}}$ is the
unique global minimizer. Each one-hot vertex $\mathbf{e}_k$ satisfies
$\mathbf{e}_k \in \mathcal{W}$ by construction. Since $\mathbf{w}_{\mathrm{opt}}$
minimizes IEE over all of $\mathcal{W}$, it must achieve a value no greater than the
objective at any specific feasible point, including every vertex:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{IEE}(\mathbf{w}_{\mathrm{opt}}) \leq \mathrm{IEE}(\mathbf{e}_k)
  \quad \forall k \in \{1, \ldots, K\}
  \label{eq:dom2}
\end{equation}
Taking the minimum of the right-hand side over $k$ preserves the inequality:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{IEE}(\mathbf{w}_{\mathrm{opt}}) \leq \min_{k \in \{1,\ldots,K\}} \mathrm{IEE}(\mathbf{e}_k)
  \label{eq:dom3}
\end{equation}
This establishes that the optimally weighted ensemble is at least as effective as the
best single constituent mechanism in minimizing the IEE objective.\hfill$\square$
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Regularisation Convergence]
The actual optimization objective implements
$J(\mathbf{w}) = \mathrm{IEE}(\mathbf{w}) - \lambda_{\mathrm{reg}}\mathcal{H}(\mathbf{w})$.
Since $\mathcal{H}(\mathbf{w})$ is bounded on the compact simplex $\mathcal{W}$, $J$ is
continuous in $\lambda_{\mathrm{reg}}$; as $\lambda_{\mathrm{reg}} \to 0$,
$J(\mathbf{w}^*) \to \mathrm{IEE}(\mathbf{w}_{\mathrm{opt}})$ by continuity of the
minimiser. Consequently, the regularised optimum deliberately trades a marginal IEE
increase for enforced weight diversity $\mathcal{H}$, establishing generalisation across
disparate network topologies while remaining bounded by Proposition~1.
\end{corollary}

\section{Numerical Scale Analysis and Empirical Findings}
\label{sec:results}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

Simulation results are presented across three topologies and four network sizes
($n \in [50, 500]$), validating the Ensemble Dominance Proposition and quantifying the
IEE reduction achieved by ELAPSE relative to individual mechanisms and the uncontrolled
baseline.

As evidenced by Figure~\ref{fig:entropy}, which plots the $H(t)$ phase trajectories, the
M0 theoretical baseline exhibits information entropy climbing toward the asymptotic
maximum ($\ln(n)$), whereas the localized reactive thresholds limit overall data
expansion.

\textbf{Comparison with chronological timeout protocols.}
The M0 baseline provides a principled upper bound on the IEE accumulated by any static,
time-blind protocol such as Vanish~\cite{geambasu2009vanish} or the
Ephemerizer~\cite{perlman2005ephemerizer}. To make this comparison concrete, we
simulate a hard-timeout baseline (denoted $M_{\mathrm{TO}}$) that mirrors the Vanish
operational model: erasure is triggered uniformly at a fixed global timeout
$T^* = 0.5T$, with no spatial reactivity prior to that point. Under this model, the
network accumulates IEE identically to M0 for $t < T^*$, after which the data mass
collapses to zero. For $n = 500$ on the ER topology, this yields a conservative upper
bound of
$\mathrm{IEE}(M_{\mathrm{TO}}) \lesssim \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot\mathrm{IEE}(\mathrm{M0})
\approx 3527.9$ (the true value is lower since $H(t) \cdot M(t)$ grows superlinearly,
so the second half of the interval contributes disproportionately more than the
first)---still $7.5\times$ higher than the ELAPSE ensemble (470.4). Critically, on
scale-free BA topologies where viral diffusion saturates a large fraction of nodes well
before any fixed global timeout---as evidenced by the rapid early-phase entropy climb
visible in Figure~\ref{fig:entropy}---the timeout protocol offers no containment during
the entire explosive growth phase, whereas ELAPSE activates reactively as soon as
$H(t) > H_c$. This demonstrates that the IEE improvement of ELAPSE is not merely
relative to an uncontrolled theoretical bound, but represents a fundamental structural
advantage over the chronological paradigm.

\section{Conclusions}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

Controlling digital proliferation without rigid static constraints requires mathematical
spatial analysis. The ELAPSE framework proposes an alternative to chronological
timestamps, building a reactive structural limit sourced from selected natural mechanics.
By dynamically learning optimal topological parameters, ELAPSE reduced the IEE by 93.3\%
relative to the uncontrolled baseline at $n = 500$ (decreasing from 7055.7 to 470.4).

Despite these promising results, the framework possesses inherent limitations. The
stochastic-finance vote ($v_3$) historically underperforms because the
Ornstein-Uhlenbeck barrier approximation serves as a marked simplification of true
complex-network diffusion. Furthermore, Proposition~1 rigorously bounds the ensemble
dominance only for the unregularised objective, necessitating practical trade-offs.
Finally, the present validation is conducted entirely via numerical simulation on
synthetic graph topologies; future work must establish deployment on a live, volatile
cryptographic peer-to-peer network to confirm empirical parity.

Future work will focus on integrating dynamic neural weight prediction to bypass the
static overhead of the Nelder-Mead simplex solver, extending the Laplacian operators to
accommodate directed social graphs, and establishing a formal well-posedness proof for
the underlying stochastic differential systems driving the erasure boundary.
